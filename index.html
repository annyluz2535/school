    CHOCÓ

    Chocó is one of the thirty-two departments of Colombia, located in the northwest of the country, in the Colombian Pacific region. It includes the forests of the Darién and the basins of the Atrato and San Juan rivers. Its capital is the city of Quibdó. It is the only department in Colombia with coasts on the Pacific and Atlantic oceans. It is also the only department bordering Panama. It is home to the ecoregion that probably has the highest rainfall on the planet.4 Broadly speaking, it comprises half of the national coastline in the Pacific Ocean.

    The department of Chocó offers innumerable tourist attractions from the natural, cultural and scientific point of view. Among the main tourist attractions, the Los Katíos, Utría and Tatamá natural national parks stand out. Jungles and virgin beaches offer visitors the most exotic flora and fauna of the tropics: mangroves with exclusive habitats. Thermal springs sprout from its volcanic soils that allow it to be compared with certain areas of Hawaii. Its calm waters allow sports such as skiing, diving, fishing and sailing sports, among others. The charm of the Pacific, with wide beaches and landscapes of contrasting vegetation.     A little historical review!

    During pre-Columbian times the territory is inhabited by the Kuna or Tule peoples in the Gulf of Urabá and lower Atrato; the Wounaan or Noanamaes in the San Juan and the Emberás, Baudoes or Citararaes of Alto Atrato and Baudo.5 In 1501 Rodrigo de Bastidas explored the territory and Martín Fernández de Enciso made the first foundation of a European city on the American continent: Santa María la Antigua del Darién in 1510. The city was abandoned in 1525 due to disputes between the conquerors and the Kunas.
    On September 25, 1513, Europeans discovered the Pacific Ocean and called it the South Sea. The Spanish start the African slave trade in America. In 1728 Barule, a slave, led the biggest insurrection in Chocó with the brothers Antonio and Mateo Mina. These insurgents founded the Palenque de Tadó where Barule was proclaimed king. It is made up of 120 maroons. On February 18, 1728, the battle between the royalist army and the Maroons took place and, defeated by the latter, Barule and the Mina brothers were shot by Lieutenant Tres Palacios Mier.     Chocoanos young or old also love, enjoy, and take pride in their locally served main meals. Among the main dishes of Chocó are: Sancocho, a rich soup composed of meats, vegetables, cheese from the Caribbean coast, yucca, banana and potatoes - generally prepared and eaten with rice and natural juice of local fruits such as lulo, pineapple, lemon and guavas. They also love to prepare and eat Bochinche rice, a rice-like meal made up of meats, vegetables, and cheese.


    Cultural Social Identity

    El Chocó is predominantly an Afro-Colombian department, where 90% of the local population identifies as Black, 6% as Indigenous, and 4% as Mestizo or White. The main cities of Chocó, Istmina and Quibdó are more ethnically diverse than most of the cities I have visited in Colombia. However, while these ethnic identities and diversities are beautiful, they also contribute to some of the root causes of political, economic and social injustices within Choco and throughout Colombia. Over the years, these ethnic differences have shaped Chocó's relationship with the Colombian government as demonstrated by recent regional strikes. However, all Chocoanos Indígenas, Negroes, Blancos, or Mestizos are very proud of their department, their nationality as Colombians, and their ability to live together as neighbors.
